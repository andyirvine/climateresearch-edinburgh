## INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

```
I: Fiona, thank you for meeting me. I know from our initial contact that you're quite involved in climate-related activity. Can we start with when that started for you — has this always been important to you?

R: It's been somewhere in my consciousness since the nineties, honestly. I trained as a nurse and you end up quite attuned to public health, and environmental health is part of that. But it's ebbed and flowed in terms of how much headspace I've given it. When my children were young I was more focused on them. When I retired three years ago, I had more time and more urgency, I suppose.

I: What triggered the urgency?

R: A few things. The floods in 2023 were quite frightening — we weren't personally affected but the news coverage was. And then my granddaughter was born, and something shifted. She's only three now but — you look at a three-year-old and you think, what is the world going to be like when she's thirty? I couldn't just leave that as an abstract worry.

I: So you became more active?

R: I joined Transition Edinburgh about two years ago. I'd been aware of them for a long time but hadn't taken the step. A friend dragged me along to one of their repair cafés and I met the people and thought, this is what I want to be doing. It's not hair-shirt environmentalism — it's very practical, very positive, very welcoming. We do repair workshops, local food events, a community composting scheme. It doesn't feel like sacrifice; it feels like community.

I: For people who don't know Transition Edinburgh — what is it?

R: It's part of the wider Transition Towns movement — you might know it from Totnes in Devon, that's where it started. The idea is local communities building resilience and reducing dependence on fossil fuels from the bottom up, rather than waiting for governments. The Edinburgh branch is well established, it's been going for years. There's a real breadth of people involved — not just the usual suspects you might expect.

I: How do you think about climate change in terms of what's most urgent to you?

R: What worries me most is the pace of change. We're not moving fast enough. And I do worry about tipping points — permafrost, ice caps — things that once they go, they're gone. But I try not to get trapped in catastrophism because that's paralysing. I prefer to focus on what's achievable locally.

I: Where do you get your information?

R: I read The Guardian — I know, very predictable. [laughs] But also more scientific sources. I follow the IPCC updates, I read the Scottish Government's climate plan. Transition Edinburgh sends out very good briefings. And I listen to a lot of podcasts — there are some good ones on climate solutions, not just problems.

I: Does climate change come up much in your social circle?

R: Yes, quite a bit. My friends are mostly of a similar mind. I'm probably in a bit of a bubble, I acknowledge that. My sister is less engaged — she lives in a more rural area, funny enough, and she's more focused on the cost-of-living pressures. We talk about it but not always easily.

I: Have you noticed climate impacts directly in Edinburgh or the surrounding area?

R: The Water of Leith flooding. The changes in the seasons — it sounds minor but as someone who grew up here, you notice that spring comes earlier, certain birds and plants are shifting. The snow. I remember proper snows as a child; my granddaughter may never know what a real Scottish winter was. That saddens me.

I: You mentioned Scotland specifically — does your sense of Scottish identity connect to how you feel about this?

R: Yes, deeply. There's something specifically Scottish about how I feel this. It's our landscape, our rivers, our coasts, our hills. When those change, it feels like a loss of something that belongs to us. And I think Scotland has real potential to lead on this — we have the renewables resources, we have the political will to some extent. I find it frustrating when that potential isn't matched with action.

I: You mean at the Scottish Government level?

R: Yes. The ambitions have been good but the delivery has been patchy. The 2030 targets were missed. That's disappointing when you believe in what they were trying to do. Though I should say — I don't think any government is fully meeting the challenge.

I: What would you say to someone from outside this community about how older Edinburgh residents relate to climate change?

R: I'd say don't underestimate us. There's a tendency to frame climate action as a young person's issue, and that's wrong. Many people my age and older have a deep connection to place, to nature, to the long view — we remember what things were like before. We have a lot to offer. What we sometimes need is to be invited rather than lectured at.
```
