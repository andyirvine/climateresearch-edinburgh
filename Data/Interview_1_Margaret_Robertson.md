## MANUAL NOTES

### Insights

1. Margaret distinguishes clearly between accepting climate change as real and opposing specific policy responses she considers economically harmful — particularly around heat pump mandates and the pace of EV transition. This tension may be common among older, property-owning conservatives.
2. She is far more emotionally engaged with climate when framed around her grandchildren's futures than when framed around abstract statistics or political targets. The grandparent frame was a clear entry point.

### Opportunities

- Messaging that connects intergenerational responsibility to practical, incremental action (rather than systemic overhaul) may resonate well.
- Her trust in the BBC and broadsheet journalism suggests established media remains credible with this demographic — contrast to online sceptic content.

## INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

```
I: Thank you for making time for this, Margaret. I'd like to start broadly — how often would you say climate change comes up in your day-to-day life, whether in conversation or in the news?

R: It comes up quite a lot in the news, doesn't it. Barely a week goes by without something on the BBC or in The Times about it. In conversation — less so, I'd say. We might mention extreme weather when it happens, or if there's something on the news that's particularly alarming. But it's not something my friends and I sit down and discuss over dinner, no.

I: What's your general sense of it — do you feel concerned about climate change?

R: I do, yes. I think I'd be a fool not to. The evidence is pretty overwhelming at this point, isn't it. I'm not a denier. I do sometimes feel that certain political parties have attached themselves to it in ways that feel more ideological than practical, and that puts me off. But the underlying science — I accept that.

I: Can you say a bit more about what concerns you specifically?

R: The weather patterns changing is something I notice. We used to have proper cold winters here in Edinburgh, and now they're much milder, and then you get these sudden burst events — very heavy rainfall, flooding. We had a terrible flood in Morningside a few years back, and my neighbour in the next street — her entire ground floor was destroyed. She was in her seventies and had to move out for six months while it was repaired. That felt very real. That wasn't some future scenario; that was happening now.

I: Has that changed how you think about it?

R: Yes, I think it has. It made it less abstract. Before, I thought of climate change as something that would affect my grandchildren, not me. Now I think it's already here in some ways.

I: You mentioned your grandchildren — how old are they?

R: Seven and nine. A boy and a girl.

I: Do they ever talk to you about climate change?

R: My granddaughter does, actually. She's nine and she's learning about it at school. She asked me once what the summers would be like when she's my age. I didn't know what to say. That bothered me more than any government report, honestly. You don't want to frighten children, but you also don't want to lie to them.

I: Where do you personally get most of your information about climate change?

R: BBC primarily. The Ten O'Clock News, Radio 4. I read The Times — I know some people would say that skews things a certain way but I find it reasonably balanced on the science, even if the commentators vary. I'd say I'm fairly well-informed, but I'm not someone who reads academic journals or attends talks.

I: Are there sources you actively distrust?

R: Social media, largely. I'm on Facebook to keep up with friends and family, and you see the most extraordinary things being shared — videos claiming the whole thing is a hoax or that the sun is responsible, not human activity. I find that frustrating. I'm not easily taken in by it, but I can see how older people in particular might be, if they're spending a lot of time on those platforms.

I: What's your sense of how people in your community here in Morningside feel about it?

R: I think most people accept it's happening. This is an educated area, a lot of retired professionals. I don't hear outright denial. What I do hear is frustration with the policy responses. People grumbling about the heat pump schemes, or the ULEZ-type proposals, or the cost of living being made worse by green taxes. There's a sense of — fine, we accept the problem, but are these really the right solutions, and why should ordinary people bear the cost?

I: Do you share those frustrations?

R: To some extent. I believe it's happening. I just don't always believe the solutions they're selling us are the right ones. I had someone come and do an assessment on this house for a heat pump. The quote was quite extraordinary. I'm not poor but that's not something I could absorb easily, and I'm a widow now so it's just my income. I feel the system isn't really designed for people like me.

I: Are you aware of any organisations in Edinburgh that work on climate change at a community level?

R: I've heard of Transition Edinburgh, vaguely. I've seen posters. I haven't been involved in it. There's also something through the church — St Cuthbert's down on Lothian Road has done some environmental events, I believe, though I don't attend there myself. The Council has a climate strategy of some kind. I'm not actively engaged with any of them.

I: Would you want to be?

R: If it was practical. I'm not really one for activism. But if there were community conversations — not lectures, genuine conversations — I'd attend something like that. I'm not disengaged. I just haven't been invited in, if that makes sense.

I: Is there anything you'd want people researching this topic to understand about how you and people like you relate to climate change?

R: I think — don't write us off as not caring because we're not carrying placards. Most people my age have seen enormous changes in our lifetimes and we do worry about the world our grandchildren will inherit. We just want practical honesty rather than catastrophising or being made to feel guilty for having a gas boiler. That's all.
```
