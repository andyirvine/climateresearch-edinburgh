## MANUAL NOTES

### Insights

1. Bob is the clearest example in this sample of a respondent who has migrated toward climate scepticism via online media — specifically YouTube channels and Facebook groups that promote alternative scientific narratives. His engineering background gives him confidence in his ability to evaluate technical claims, which makes him harder to reach via conventional messaging.
2. Despite his scepticism about causes, he does not deny that change is occurring and is genuinely concerned about the sea at Portobello and the wellbeing of his grandchildren. The environmental observations are a possible bridge.

### Opportunities

- Bob's engineering and technical literacy means he responds to data, evidence, and mechanism — not moral appeals. If engaging this demographic, technical credibility is essential.
- His love of Portobello beach and the Firth is a visceral, personal anchor. Local environmental changes are more persuasive to him than global narratives.

### Key quotes

> "I'm not saying nothing's changing. Something's changing. I just don't accept this idea that it's all down to us driving our cars. The sun's been doing things for four billion years."

> "I've swum at Portobello since I was a boy. The water's different. The storms are different. Whether that's CO2 or something else — I genuinely don't know."

> "I don't want my grandkids inheriting a mess. I want that on the record. I'm not against doing the right thing. I just want to be sure we know what the right thing is."

## INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

```
I: Thanks for agreeing to this, Bob. I'll just dive in — is climate change something you follow closely?

R: I follow it, aye. I've got an engineering background — I spent thirty years in structural engineering — so when I hear technical claims I want to look into them rather than just accept them. I follow it because I'm curious about the science, not because I necessarily agree with the consensus.

I: What do you mean by that?

R: I think the picture is more complicated than what you get in the mainstream media. There are scientists — not crackpots, real scientists — who question how much of the warming is anthropogenic versus natural variation. Solar cycles, ocean cycles, things like that. I've read quite a bit of it. I don't think it's as settled as they say.

I: Where have you been reading that?

R: Various places. There are channels online — YouTube, some forums. Some independent scientists who've been marginalised by the mainstream. I know that sounds like I'm going down a rabbit hole, but I try to read the actual data, not just the headlines.

I: Are there mainstream sources you trust as well?

R: I'll watch the BBC, aye. I read papers sometimes. But I take everything with a degree of scepticism. That's just how I'm trained to think. You don't build a bridge on assumptions; you test them.

I: Have you noticed any changes in the environment around Portobello over the years?

R: [pauses] Aye. I'll be honest with you. I've swum at that beach since I was a boy. My father used to take me down in the summer. The sea is different now. The storms that come in off the Firth — they're heavier, they come at different times of year. The beach itself has changed shape. Sand moved around a lot more than it used to. Now, whether that's CO2 or something else, I genuinely don't know. But something's different.

I: Does that concern you?

R: It does. The beach is part of who I am, if that doesn't sound daft. I still swim there — I've done the Portobello Loony Dook for fifteen years. If that changes, if the water becomes dangerous or the beach is lost, that would be a real loss to me personally.

I: You have four grandchildren. Do you think about this in terms of their futures?

R: That's where it gets complicated for me. Of course I do. I'm not a callous man. I want them to have a good world. But I also worry that we might be making enormous economic sacrifices based on modelling that isn't as reliable as they claim. If you wreck the economy trying to fix the climate and the science turns out to be partly wrong, you've done huge damage for nothing.

I: Does climate change come up in conversation with your grandchildren?

R: The sixteen-year-old, yes. She's very firm about it — all down to humans, the science is settled, full stop. We've had some rows. [laughs] I try to be careful about it because I don't want to undermine what they're being taught at school, but I also think — you should be able to question things. That's not a bad lesson.

I: How has that felt — having different views from your granddaughter?

R: Fine, mostly. She's a smart girl. She challenges me and I challenge her back. I think that's healthy. She did once say she was scared about the future, and that — that got to me. I don't want my grandkids lying awake worrying. I want that on the record. I'm not against doing the right thing. I just want to be sure we know what the right thing is.

I: Are you aware of any organisations in Edinburgh working on climate issues?

R: I know there are groups. I've seen Extinction Rebellion types in the city centre, which I find — not to my taste. More theatrical than useful. I think I've seen something about a local community group in Portobello that does environmental work — beach cleaning, that sort of thing. That I'd be more open to. Practical things I understand.

I: Would you rule out engaging with any of that?

R: If it was practically focused — beach preservation, local flooding — aye, I'd look at it. If it was political activism I'd probably give it a miss. I'm a practical man.

I: Is there anything you'd want to say about how you relate to this topic that you think gets missed?

R: People assume that if you ask questions about the science you don't care. That's wrong. I care. I just care enough to want to get it right rather than go along with something because it's fashionable. There are a lot of people my age who feel the same. We've seen fashions come and go. That doesn't mean we're right to be sceptical, but it doesn't mean we're stupid either.
```
