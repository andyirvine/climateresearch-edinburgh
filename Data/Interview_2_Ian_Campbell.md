## MANUAL NOTES

### Insights

1. Ian's relationship to climate change is mediated heavily through class identity and economic insecurity. He accepts the science but resents what he perceives as middle-class environmentalism that ignores working-class communities. This is a distinct attitudinal pattern worth noting.
2. His strongest emotional connection to climate is via his 14-year-old granddaughter, who is very engaged with the topic — suggesting intergenerational transmission is active in this household even among more sceptical grandparents.

### Opportunities

- Working-class community framing — linking climate action to jobs, health, and housing rather than environmental ethics — may be the most effective entry point for respondents like Ian.
- Leith community organisations (particularly those with roots in the area's industrial and maritime heritage) may offer credible messengers for this demographic.

### Key quotes

> "I'm not saying it's not happening. I'm saying the people who can least afford it are always the ones being asked to change, and the ones who caused it fly about in private jets and tell us to insulate our lofts."

> "My granddaughter was at a school strike a couple of years back. I didn't stop her. I was actually quite proud of her, though I'd never tell her that."

## INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

```
I: Thanks for sitting down with me, Ian. Can I ask — is climate change something you think about much, day to day?

R: Aye, it comes up. You can't avoid it really, can you, it's always on the telly. Whether I think about it in my own life — that's a different question. I've got enough to think about without adding the end of the world to the list. [laughs]

I: Fair enough. What's your general view on it — do you think it's a serious problem?

R: Look, I'm not a scientist, but from what I can tell, aye, it's happening. The weather's changed. I've lived here my whole life and the winters are softer than they used to be, and the storms when they come are heavier. My dad used to take me down to Leith docks as a boy and we'd watch the Firth in all weathers. I know what it used to look like. It's different now. So I'm not going to sit here and tell you it's all made up.

I: But?

R: [laughs] But I've got reservations about the whole circus around it. It's become very political, and not always in ways that benefit people like me. There's a certain type of person who's very loud about climate change and they're usually quite comfortable financially, and somehow the solutions always seem to involve making energy more expensive or telling people they shouldn't drive their cars, and those people are the ones who'll feel it least.

I: That frustration — is that widely shared among people you know?

R: Round here? Aye, quite widely. This is not a wealthy part of Edinburgh. We've got people in fuel poverty. We've got families who can barely heat their homes. And you've got someone on the telly telling them they should get a heat pump. It's — disconnected. It feels disconnected from reality.

I: Where do you get most of your information about climate change?

R: Telly, mostly. BBC News, STV. I'll read the Evening News sometimes, online. I'm not really a newspaper person. My son-in-law sends me things on WhatsApp, some of it I don't know what to do with — half of it seems to be conspiracy rubbish, I ignore that. But the mainstream news, aye, that's where I get most of it.

I: Does it come up in conversation with friends or family?

R: It does with my granddaughter, actually. She's fourteen and she's very into it. She was at one of those school strikes a couple of years back. I didn't stop her. I was actually quite proud of her, though I'd never admit that to her face — she'd never let me hear the end of it. [laughs] She talks to me about it quite seriously. I try to listen. She knows more about the details than I do.

I: Does that change your perspective at all, hearing it from her?

R: A wee bit, aye. She makes it feel more personal. Less like something being done to you by a government and more like something that actually matters. I'd rather she didn't have to be worrying about it at fourteen, but that's the world she's growing up in.

I: What about the other grandchildren — the younger ones?

R: The four-year-old hasn't got a clue, obviously. [laughs] The eleven-year-old hears about it at school. He's mentioned it. It seems like they do quite a bit of it in school now, which I think is good, as long as it's not just doom and gloom, you know. You don't want to terrify the kids.

I: Have you noticed any direct impacts of climate change in Leith or the surrounding area?

R: The flooding has been bad a few times. The Water of Leith burst its banks a few years back and there was damage around here — not on my street but nearby. And the sea — you can see it in the Firth. The storms come in differently than they did. I'm not a scientist, but you don't need to be to notice that.

I: Are you aware of any organisations in Edinburgh that work on climate or environmental issues?

R: There's something at the foot of the Walk — the Leith Community Centre does some environmental things, I've seen flyers. There's a community garden up at Pilrig that does stuff around sustainability. I don't know the names off the top of my head. The Council has all sorts of initiatives, but whether they actually get down to this level is another question.

I: Would you engage with any of those?

R: If it was something concrete and practical — like reducing energy costs or something that actually helped people — aye, I'd look at it. If it was people standing around discussing their feelings about the planet, probably not. I'm practical. Show me the benefit.

I: Last question — is there anything you'd want to say to someone researching this topic?

R: Aye. Don't forget that for a lot of people, climate change isn't the most urgent crisis they're facing. It's real and it matters, but so does the fact that folk can't pay their bills. The two things need to be connected. If you're asking people to sacrifice more, you need to make sure it's not always the same people making the sacrifices.
```
