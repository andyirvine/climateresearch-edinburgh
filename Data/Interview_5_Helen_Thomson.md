## INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

```
I: Helen, thank you for speaking with me. You've had a career in ecology, so you're coming to this with a different lens than most. Can you say a bit about that?

R: I spent thirty-odd years teaching ecology and environmental science at Heriot-Watt. So yes, I've watched this issue evolve from a theoretical concern to an observable reality over the course of my career. It's been quite something to live through professionally. Not always comfortable.

I: What do you mean by not always comfortable?

R: I think anyone who understands the science in any depth has to grapple with what it means to know what we know. I understand the feedback loops, the tipping points, the lag time between action and effect. Some days that's almost unbearable. You want to shout at people — not because they're bad people, but because the gap between what the science tells us and what society is doing about it is so vast.

I: How do you manage that?

R: Better than I used to, honestly. I had a period a few years back — I'd call it eco-anxiety, it's a real thing — where I was genuinely struggling to function. I got help, I did some therapy, I talked to colleagues who felt the same. Now I try to focus on what I can do, what's within my sphere of influence. The grandchildren help. They give you a reason to stay engaged rather than despair.

I: Tell me about the grandchildren.

R: Five and eight. They're wonderful. The eight-year-old is learning about climate at school — she comes home with questions and I love it. She knows I know things about nature and she asks me everything. The five-year-old isn't there yet but he loves being outside, loves animals. I take them both out quite a bit — the Meadows, Holyrood Park, sometimes the Botanic Gardens. I want them to have a relationship with the natural world. I think that matters.

I: Does it come up directly — climate change — with them?

R: With the eight-year-old, yes. She's asked me if animals will go extinct, whether the sea will flood our house — they ask these big questions. I try to be honest without being frightening. My grandchildren still think I can fix everything because I know about plants and animals. I can't fix this. But I try not to let them see that it frightens me. That's probably not entirely sustainable but I do my best.

I: What do you observe in Edinburgh itself — evidence of change?

R: The phenology is remarkable. Phenology is the timing of natural events — when birds arrive, when plants flower. The blackbirds in my garden come a month earlier than they did when I moved to this house in 1999. That's not a statistic from a paper; that's something I've watched with my own eyes. The street trees are showing signs of stress in hot summers that we never used to have. The Water of Leith has flooded more frequently and severely. The combination of mild winters and heavy precipitation events is a classic signature of what the models predicted.

I: Where do you keep up with developments now you're not in academia?

R: I still read the journals — old habits. I follow Carbon Brief very closely, that's an excellent resource for anyone who wants serious science communication. The Guardian does good climate reporting. I'm on some academic mailing lists. I also do a lot through Friends of the Earth Scotland — I volunteer with them and we do a fair bit of keeping across policy developments.

I: Can you tell me about Friends of the Earth Scotland?

R: It's the Scottish branch of FoES — it's well established, with campaigns on everything from oil licensing to climate justice to just transition for workers in the fossil fuel sector. I got involved after I retired because I had time and I wanted the engagement to be more than reading papers alone. They're good at connecting local action to national and international policy. There are other organisations too — Stop Climate Chaos Scotland is a coalition, and of course Transition Edinburgh does great community-level work. I know several people who work across both.

I: How do you find people in your social circles relate to climate change compared to how you relate to it?

R: There's a spectrum. Many of my friends are similarly engaged — academics, professionals, people who've thought about this. But there's also a set of people, even intelligent and educated people, who've somehow decided the problem is too big so they'll focus elsewhere, or who've become somewhat numbed to it. That numbness worries me more than active denial, actually. Denial you can engage with. Numbness is harder.

I: Is there a message you'd want people researching this topic to take away?

R: Two things. One — the emotional dimension is real and underresearched. Eco-anxiety, grief for lost environments, the psychological cost of knowing — this affects a lot of people, especially older people who remember what things used to be like. It deserves proper attention. Two — older people are not a lost cause. We have time, we have knowledge, many of us have resources. We can be allies. But you have to engage with us as people with agency, not just as subjects to be communicated at.
```
