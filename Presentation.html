Attitudes
The Core Finding:
The Acceptance / Policy Gap
4/5
Accept the science
1/5
Trust current policy responses

The dominant attitudinal pattern is not denial — it is a gap between accepting that climate change is real and trusting that the policy response is fair, practical, or well-designed.

This legitimacy gap is shaped by concerns about individual cost, distributional fairness, and political capture. It is a more tractable challenge than outright denial — but it requires different messaging.

"I believe it's happening. I just don't always believe the solutions they're selling us are the right ones." — Margaret, 68, Conservative
"I'm not saying it's not happening. I'm saying the people who can least afford it are always the ones being asked to change." — Ian, 74, Labour
"Denial you can engage with. Numbness is harder." — Helen, 65, Green

Impacts
What People Have Observed Locally
4/5
Water of Leith flooding
The most cited local climate signal. Repeated flooding of a well-known city waterway has made climate change feel present, not future.
2/5
Changes to the Firth of Forth
Ian and Bob — both with lifelong connections to the waterfront — note heavier storms, changed sea conditions, and coastal erosion.
2/5
Seasonal & ecological shifts
Earlier springs, loss of snowfall, changes in bird arrival dates. Scientifically documented by Helen; felt impressionistically by Fiona.
Personal memory as environmental evidence
"The blackbirds in my garden come a month earlier than they did when I moved to this house. That's not a statistic. That's something I've watched with my own eyes." — Helen
"I've swum at Portobello since I was a boy. The water's different. The storms are different." — Bob (sceptic on causes, not on change)

Even the most sceptical respondent does not deny that change is occurring. Long-term personal observation is a form of local environmental knowledge — an asset, not just anecdote.

Why Transition Edinburgh Stands Out

Known even by non-members. Valued by active participants. Its model — repair cafés, community food, composting — lowers the barrier through practical framing rather than political identity.

"It's not hair-shirt environmentalism — it's very practical, very positive, very welcoming. It doesn't feel like sacrifice; it feels like community." — Fiona, active member
Principle across all five respondents
Practical, community-level activity is preferred over advocacy or protest — regardless of political leaning.
Cross-Cutting Themes
Five Themes Across the Sample
① Grandchildren as emotional bridge ▾
Present in all 5 interviews. Grandchildren function as information sources, emotional anchors, mirrors of legacy, and — in Bob's case — active challengers of his scepticism. The grandparent-grandchild relationship is the single most consistent lever for climate engagement in this sample.
② Practical action preferred over activism ▾
Four of five respondents prefer concrete, community-level activity to political advocacy or street protest. Margaret wants "genuine conversations, not lectures." Ian wants to "see the benefit." Bob will consider beach preservation but not political protest. This preference cuts across the political spectrum.
③ Class and economic justice ▾
The costs of transition are perceived as falling on those least able to afford them. This concern is not unique to Ian (working-class, Labour) — Margaret (Conservative, property-owning) also describes being unable to absorb heat pump costs as a widow on a fixed income. Distributional fairness is a cross-partisan issue.
④ Scottish place attachment ▾
Three respondents connect concern for climate to a personal relationship with Scotland's specific landscape. Fiona makes this explicit; Ian and Bob express it through attachment to the Firth of Forth. Localised, place-specific framing — Scotland's coasts, the Water of Leith, Edinburgh's seasons — resonates more than global narratives.
⑤ Online scepticism and the limits of media literacy ▾
Bob's trajectory shows how professional confidence can increase susceptibility to sophisticated counter-narratives. His engineering background makes him trust his own evaluation of technical claims — which has led him toward sceptic YouTube content he frames as "marginalised science." Margaret and Ian are aware of this vulnerability; Bob is not.

Implications for Engagement
Seven Recommendations
  • 1
    Lead with local. The Water of Leith, the Firth, Edinburgh's changing seasons are more resonant than global statistics.
  • 2
    Use the grandparent-grandchild dynamic. School-based learning is already priming these conversations — community engagement can build on it deliberately.
  • 3
    Practical beats political. Transition Edinburgh's model — repair, composting, food — is a template worth examining and scaling.
  • 4
    Distinguish policy sceptics from science deniers. Most of this sample accepts the science. Frustration with policy is a different and more tractable challenge.
  • 5
    Integrate economic justice. Who pays and who benefits cannot be an afterthought. For fixed-income and working-class respondents this is inseparable from the climate question.
  • 6
    Explore faith communities and hyper-local groups. St Cuthbert's, local beach and garden groups — credible, low-barrier entry points for those who won't self-select into environmental organisations.
  • 7
    Acknowledge the emotional dimension. Eco-anxiety, environmental grief, and psychological burden are present and underresearched in this demographic.
Next Steps
Gaps & Further Research
What this sample can't tell us
  • 1
    Geographic gaps. All five respondents live in inner Edinburgh. Perspectives from Craigmillar, Wester Hailes, and outer suburbs may diverge significantly.
  • 2
    Eco-anxiety prevalence. Helen is the only respondent to name this explicitly. Structured follow-up questions are needed to establish how widespread it is.
  • 3
    Transition Edinburgh's reach. Named by 3 of 5 respondents unprompted — warrants a dedicated organisational interview.
  • 4
    The online sceptic pathway. Bob's trajectory is significant. What drives it and what might interrupt it?
  • 5
    Intergenerational co-design. Given the grandparent-grandchild dynamic, activities designed for both generations together are worth piloting.
