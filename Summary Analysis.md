# Summary Analysis: Climate Change Attitudes Among Older Edinburgh Residents

**Sample:** 5 qualitative interviews
**Date range:** 12–21 February 2026
**Target group:** Edinburgh residents, ages 60–80, with grandchildren

---

## 1. Sample Overview

| # | Name | Age | Political leaning | Location | Grandchildren |
|---|------|-----|-------------------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | Margaret Robertson | 68 | Conservative / Centre-right | Morningside | 2 |
| 2 | Ian Campbell | 74 | Labour / Centre-left | Leith | 3 |
| 3 | Fiona MacDonald | 62 | SNP / Centre-left | Stockbridge | 1 |
| 4 | Bob Mitchell | 79 | No strong affiliation / Sceptical | Portobello | 4 |
| 5 | Helen Thomson | 65 | Green / Progressive left | Marchmont | 2 |

The sample spans the political spectrum, three distinct Edinburgh neighbourhoods (affluent, working-class, and mixed), and a range of engagement levels from active volunteer (Fiona, Helen) to climate sceptic (Bob). Ages range from 62 to 79.

---

## 2. Attitudes

### 2.1 The acceptance/policy gap

The most consistent finding across the sample is a gap between accepting climate change as real and supporting the specific policy responses being promoted. This is most clearly articulated by Margaret:

> *"I believe it's happening. I just don't always believe the solutions they're selling us are the right ones."*

This pattern holds, in varying forms, across four of the five respondents. Only Helen, with her scientific background and Green affiliation, expresses consistent alignment between her acceptance of the science and her support for urgent systemic action. For the others, acceptance of climate change coexists with scepticism about costs, speed, or the fairness of current policy approaches.

This should not be read as denial. It is better characterised as a legitimacy gap: respondents broadly trust the science but do not trust the policy process or the framing of solutions.

### 2.2 Political mediation of attitudes

Political identity shapes not just the level of engagement but the frame through which climate is understood:

- **Conservative framing (Margaret):** Accepts science; concerned about economic burden on individuals and the perceived ideological capture of the issue by the left.
- **Labour/working-class framing (Ian):** Accepts science; most concerned with distributive justice — who bears the cost of transition.
- **SNP/independence framing (Fiona):** Accepts science and policy urgency; connects climate action to Scottish identity and national potential.
- **Sceptical framing (Bob):** Accepts observable change but questions anthropogenic attribution; concerns centre on economic risk of premature action.
- **Green framing (Helen):** Full alignment with scientific consensus; frustrated by the pace of action at all levels.

These framings matter for communication strategy: there is no single message that will engage all five respondents equally.

### 2.3 Emotional landscape

Attitudes are not purely cognitive. Several respondents describe strong emotional dimensions:

- **Helen** names eco-anxiety explicitly and describes a period of significant psychological distress linked to her scientific knowledge.
- **Fiona** expresses grief at the anticipated loss of Scottish seasons and landscape.
- **Bob**, despite his scepticism, becomes visibly moved when his granddaughter's fear is mentioned.
- **Margaret** describes being more disturbed by her granddaughter's simple question about the future than by any policy document.

Helen's observation is worth highlighting as a methodological note: *"Denial you can engage with. Numbness is harder."* Passive disengagement — rather than active scepticism — may be the dominant attitudinal challenge in this demographic.

## 3. Learning

### 3.1 Primary information sources

| Respondent | Primary sources |
|------------|-----------------|
| Margaret | BBC (TV/Radio 4), The Times |
| Ian | BBC, STV, Edinburgh Evening News (online) |
| Fiona | The Guardian, IPCC reports, Scottish Government climate plan, podcasts, Transition Edinburgh briefings |
| Bob | BBC, YouTube channels (including sceptic content), online forums |
| Helen | Academic journals, Carbon Brief, The Guardian, academic mailing lists, Friends of the Earth Scotland |

Television news (especially BBC) is the primary information source for three of the five respondents. This is consistent with what would be expected for this age group and suggests traditional broadcast media retains significant reach and credibility with this demographic — a meaningful contrast to younger cohorts.

### 3.2 Trust and distrust

There is a notable split in how respondents relate to digital media. Margaret and Ian both actively distrust social media as a source of climate information, while acknowledging its prevalence. Bob has moved in the opposite direction — his deepened scepticism appears to have been shaped substantially by YouTube channels and online forums promoting alternative scientific narratives. He frames this as rigorous independent enquiry rather than misinformation consumption, which makes it difficult to challenge through conventional messaging.

Fiona and Helen represent a more engaged information diet, drawing on primary scientific sources and organisational briefings alongside mainstream media.

### 3.3 Intergenerational transmission

A striking and consistent pattern across all five interviews is the role of grandchildren as information conduits. In four of the five cases, grandchildren attending school were described as active sources of climate-related conversation:

- Margaret's 9-year-old granddaughter asks about future summers.
- Ian's 14-year-old granddaughter attended school strikes and briefs her grandfather.
- Bob's 16-year-old granddaughter challenges his scepticism.
- Helen's 8-year-old granddaughter brings questions home from school and treats her grandmother as an expert.

School-based climate education is clearly reaching these households and, in several cases, creating a reverse learning dynamic where grandchildren are informing grandparents rather than the other way around. This represents both an opportunity (grandchildren as trusted messengers) and a complexity (intergenerational disagreement, as seen with Bob).

## 4. Impacts

### 4.1 Observed local changes

All five respondents identify observable local changes, even Bob who is sceptical of their cause. The most commonly cited impacts are:

**Water of Leith flooding** — mentioned by Margaret, Ian, Fiona, and Helen. The repeated, visible flooding of a well-known city waterway appears to be the most salient local climate signal for Edinburgh residents in this sample. It has moved climate change from an abstract future scenario to a present reality for several respondents.

**Changes to the Firth of Forth** — mentioned by Ian and Bob, both of whom have personal, long-term relationships with the waterfront. Both note changes in storm patterns and sea conditions compared to their childhood experience.

**Seasonal and ecological shifts** — most clearly articulated by Helen, who documents phenological change (bird arrival dates, tree stress) with scientific precision. Fiona notes similar changes more impressionistically — earlier springs, the absence of proper snowfall.

**General weather pattern changes** — described by Margaret and Ian as milder winters punctuated by more intense rainfall events.

### 4.2 The significance of personal memory

A consistent feature across the sample is the weight placed on personal, longitudinal observation. Respondents with deep roots in Edinburgh or its surrounding waterways (Ian at Leith docks, Bob at Portobello beach, Helen in her Marchmont garden) frame change through what they personally remember, not through statistics. This creates a powerful and credible form of local environmental knowledge that should be treated as an asset in research and engagement, not merely as anecdote.

> *"The blackbirds in my garden come a month earlier than they did when I moved to this house. That's not a statistic. That's something I've watched with my own eyes."* — Helen

> *"I've swum at Portobello since I was a boy. The water's different."* — Bob

Even the most sceptical respondent does not deny change — only its cause.

## 5. Community Organisations

### 5.1 Organisations identified

| Organisation | Mentioned by | Engagement level |
|---|---|---|
| Transition Edinburgh | Margaret (aware), Fiona (active member), Helen (network contact) | Strong credibility across spectrum |
| Friends of the Earth Scotland (FoES) | Helen (active volunteer) | High engagement for scientifically literate respondents |
| Stop Climate Chaos Scotland | Helen (aware) | Low name recognition beyond Helen |
| Edinburgh City Council climate strategy | Margaret (aware), Ian (sceptical) | Low trust; seen as top-down |
| Leith Community Centre | Ian (aware) | Practical community credibility in working-class areas |
| Pilrig Community Garden | Ian (aware) | Local grassroots credibility |
| St Cuthbert's Church | Margaret (aware) | Faith-based entry point for conservative demographics |
| Local Portobello environmental/beach group | Bob (aware) | Practical local appeal even for sceptics |
| Extinction Rebellion | Bob (dismissive) | Actively alienating to sceptics and pragmatists |

### 5.2 Analysis

**Transition Edinburgh** emerges as the most broadly cited and credible community organisation across the sample. It is known even among respondents not engaged with it (Margaret), and actively valued by those who are (Fiona). Its practical, non-confrontational framing — repair cafés, community food events, composting — appears to lower the entry barrier compared to more explicitly political organisations. Fiona's description ("It's not hair-shirt environmentalism") captures its positioning well.

**Friends of the Earth Scotland** occupies a different niche: well-organised, policy-connected, and well-suited to scientifically literate or politically engaged respondents like Helen, but unlikely to reach Ian or Bob without significant reframing.

**Faith organisations** (St Cuthbert's) represent an underexplored entry point, particularly for older conservative or politically moderate respondents who may not attend secular community events.

**Hyper-local, practical groups** — beach cleaning at Portobello, community gardens in Leith — represent the most credible entry point for sceptical or economically focused respondents. Bob explicitly states he would engage with a local beach preservation group but not a political activist one. Ian applies the same test: *"Show me the benefit."*

**Extinction Rebellion** is the only organisation that generates actively negative responses, and notably does so even among respondents who are otherwise concerned about climate change (Bob). Its tactical approach appears to be a barrier rather than a bridge with this demographic.

## 6. Cross-Cutting Themes

### 6.1 Grandchildren as emotional bridge

The single most consistent finding across all five interviews is the emotional salience of grandchildren in shaping engagement with climate change. Even the most sceptical respondent (Bob) becomes emotionally engaged when his grandchildren's fear is mentioned. Even the most practically focused respondent (Ian) credits his 14-year-old granddaughter with making the issue feel "more personal."

This is not simply sentimentality. Grandchildren serve multiple functions in these respondents' relationship to climate:
- As **information sources**, bringing school-based learning into the household
- As **emotional anchors**, providing a concrete, loved reason to care about the long-term future
- As **mirrors**, reflecting back the respondent's own legacy and responsibility
- In Bob's case, as **interlocutors**, actively challenging a grandparent's scepticism

Any engagement strategy targeting this demographic should treat the grandparent-grandchild relationship as a primary lever.

### 6.2 Practical action versus activism

A strong preference for practical, community-level action over political activism runs through four of the five interviews. Margaret wants "genuine conversations, not lectures." Ian wants to "see the benefit." Bob will consider beach preservation but not street protest. Even Fiona, the most engaged respondent, frames her activity through the practical (repair cafés, composting) rather than the political.

Helen's observation about numbness being harder to address than denial may be partly explained by this: respondents are not passively indifferent but actively alienated by the dominant modes of climate communication they encounter.

### 6.3 Class and economic framing

Ian most explicitly articulates what appears to be a broadly shared structural concern: the costs of climate transition fall disproportionately on those least able to bear them, while the political class promoting change is insulated from its effects. This is not unique to the working-class respondent — Margaret (conservative), also discusses the prohibitive cost of heat pumps as a widow on a fixed income.

This economic justice dimension is not adequately captured by a simple left/right framing. It cuts across political identities and should be treated as a distinct analytical strand.

### 6.4 Scottish place attachment

Three of the five respondents connect their concern about climate change, at least in part, to a sense of attachment to Scotland's specific landscape, coast, and seasons. Fiona makes this most explicitly — *"There's something specifically Scottish about how I feel this"* — but the same emotional undercurrent is present in Ian's relationship to the Firth and Bob's to Portobello beach.

This suggests that localised, place-specific framing (Scotland's coasts, the Water of Leith, Edinburgh's street trees) may be more resonant with this community than global narratives.

### 6.5 Online scepticism and the limits of media literacy

Bob's trajectory — from general scepticism toward active consumption of online counter-narratives — illustrates a vulnerability in this demographic that should not be underestimated. His professional background (engineering) gives him confidence in his ability to evaluate technical claims, and this self-belief appears to have made him more, not less, susceptible to sophisticated disinformation. His framing of alternative-science YouTubers as "scientists who've been marginalised by the mainstream" is a recognisable pattern in how such content presents itself.

Margaret's comments suggest awareness of this dynamic: she notes that older people spending time on Facebook may be more susceptible to sceptic content than she is. The demographic vulnerability is acknowledged, but practical resilience to it is uneven.

## 7. Tensions and Divergences

| Theme | Aligned | Divergent |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Climate change is occurring | All five | — |
| Anthropogenic attribution | Margaret, Ian, Fiona, Helen | Bob (uncertain) |
| Policy responses are adequate | Fiona (partially), Helen | Margaret, Ian, Bob |
| Climate is an urgent priority | Helen, Fiona | Ian (competing priorities), Bob (action risk) |
| Community engagement desirable | All five | — |
| What kind of engagement | Practical (Margaret, Ian, Bob) | Political/activist (Fiona, Helen open to both) |
| Trust in online media | Active distrust (Margaret, Ian) | Active use (Bob) |

The most significant divergence is not between those who accept or reject the science (four out of five accept it), but between those who accept the urgency of current policy responses and those who do not. This is a more tractable divide than climate denial, but it requires different engagement strategies.

## 8. Implications for Engagement

1. **Lead with local.** Abstract global narratives are less effective than specific, local signals — the Water of Leith, the Firth, seasonal change. Respondents trust what they have personally witnessed over what they have been told.

2. **Grandparent-grandchild dynamics are a primary entry point.** Intergenerational framing unlocks emotional engagement even among the most reticent respondents. School-based climate education is already priming these conversations; community engagement could build on it deliberately.

3. **Practical beats political.** Across the political spectrum, practical and community-focused activity is more appealing than advocacy or protest. Transition Edinburgh's model — repair, composting, food — is worth examining as a template.

4. **Disaggregate the policy sceptics from the deniers.** The majority of respondents accept the science while questioning the policy response. These are different audiences requiring different messages. Policy frustration, particularly around cost and fairness, is a legitimate concern that warrants acknowledgement rather than dismissal.

5. **Class and economic justice must be integrated, not added on.** For working-class respondents and fixed-income respondents alike, the distributional question — who pays, who benefits — is inseparable from the climate question. Engagement that ignores economic realities will lose this audience.

6. **Faith communities and hyper-local groups are underexplored.** St Cuthbert's (mentioned unprompted by Margaret) and local neighbourhood groups (Portobello beach group, Leith community garden) represent credible, low-barrier entry points for respondents who would not self-select into more explicitly environmental organisations.

7. **Address the emotional dimension.** Eco-anxiety, environmental grief, and the psychological burden of long-term concern are real and present in this sample. Spaces that acknowledge these experiences — rather than demanding activism or cheerful optimism — may be genuinely valued.

---

## 9. Gaps and Suggested Next Steps

- **Expand the geographic spread.** All five respondents live in inner Edinburgh. Perspectives from respondents in outer Edinburgh suburbs or the city's more deprived areas (e.g. Craigmillar, Wester Hailes) may diverge significantly from this sample.
- **Explore eco-anxiety further.** Helen is the only respondent to name and discuss psychological distress explicitly. Structured questions on the emotional dimension in future interviews would help establish how prevalent this is.
- **Follow up with Transition Edinburgh.** Named by three respondents, this organisation warrants a dedicated organisational interview to understand its reach, demographics, and messaging approach.
- **Investigate the online sceptic pathway.** Bob's trajectory toward counter-narrative content online is a significant finding. A more targeted investigation of how this happens and what might interrupt it is warranted.
- **Test intergenerational engagement approaches.** Given the prominence of the grandparent-grandchild dynamic, co-design of engagement activities that deliberately involve both generations may be worth piloting.
